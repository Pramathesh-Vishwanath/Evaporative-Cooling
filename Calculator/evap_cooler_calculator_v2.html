What is an evaporative cooler?

An evaporative cooler (also called a swamp cooler or desert cooler) lowers air temperature by passing hot dry air through a wet pad. Water evaporates from the pad surface, absorbing heat from the air as latent heat of vaporisation. The air exits cooler and more humid. No refrigerant is used — the energy comes entirely from the air itself.

The process works best in hot, dry climates. In high-humidity conditions, the air is already close to saturation and cannot absorb much more moisture, so cooling is limited.

The adiabatic saturation process

In an ideal evaporative cooler, no heat is exchanged with the surroundings — all the energy for evaporation comes from the sensible heat of the air stream. This is called an adiabatic process. The key consequence:

h_out ≈ h_in (enthalpy is conserved)

In practice, enthalpy drops slightly (by ~0.5–2 kJ/kg) because the cooler housing loses some heat to the surroundings and the incoming water carries a small amount of enthalpy out as liquid. This is why we say "approximately" constant enthalpy.

Key formulas

1. Saturation pressure (Antoine equation approximation)

The saturation vapour pressure at temperature T (°C):

P_sat(T) = 0.6108 × exp( 17.27 × T / (T + 237.3) ) [kPa]

2. Humidity ratio from RH

HR = 0.622 × (RH/100 × P_sat) / (P_atm − RH/100 × P_sat) [kg/kg] HR_g = HR × 1000 [g/kg]

Since RHin is from the trusted inlet sensor, HRin derived this way is also reliable. HRout,measured from the outlet sensor is suspect — capacitive RH sensors drift at high humidity (>85%).

3. Specific enthalpy of moist air

h = Cp,a × Tdb + HR × (hfg,0 + Cp,v × Tdb) [kJ/kg dry air] Where: Cp,a = 1.005 kJ/kg·K (specific heat of dry air) Cp,v = 1.805 kJ/kg·K (specific heat of water vapour) hfg,0 = 2501 kJ/kg (latent heat of vaporisation at 0°C)

4. Mass flow rate of air

Va = A × v [m³/s] (volume flow = sweep area × velocity) Ma = Va × ρa [kg/s] (mass flow = volume flow × air density)

Outlet RH correction (no psychrometric chart needed)

Since Tdb,out from the thermometer is reliable but RHout from the humidity sensor is not, we reconstruct the true outlet state using the adiabatic assumption (h = const) and the known Tdb,out:

Step 1 — Solve for corrected HRout:
Set hout = hin in the enthalpy equation and solve for HR:
HRcorr = (hin − Cp,a × Tdb,out) / (hfg,0 + Cp,v × Tdb,out) [kg/kg]
Step 2 — Get corrected RHout:
Use the psychrometric RH formula with the corrected HR and Psat at Tdb,out:
RHcorr = HRcorr × Patm / ((0.622 + HRcorr) × Psat(Tdb,out)) × 100 [%]

This is pure algebra — no psychrometric chart is needed. The chart would give the same answer visually: find hin on the chart, follow that constant-enthalpy line until it crosses Tdb,out, and read off the resulting RH and HR at that intersection point.

Method 1 — Humidity ratio difference

The moisture picked up by the air stream equals the mass of water evaporated. If we know how much the humidity ratio increases and how much air flows through:

Mw,evap = (HRout − HRin) × Ma [kg water / s] Convert to g/hr: × 1000 × 3600

Method 1a uses the measured HRout (from the suspect RH sensor) — likely to overestimate.

Method 1b uses the corrected HRout (from the h = const calculation) — more reliable.

Method 2 — Sensible heat balance

The sensible heat lost by the air (it cools from Tin to Tout) must equal the latent + sensible heat absorbed by the evaporating water. The full energy balance:

Ma × Cp,a × (Tdb,in − Tdb,out) = Mw,evap × (hfg + Cp,w × (Twb,in − Tw,in)) Solving for Mw,evap: Mw,evap = [Ma × Cp,a × (Tin − Tout)] / [hfg + Cp,w × (Twb,in − Tw,in)] Where: hfg ≈ 2440 kJ/kg (latent heat of vaporisation at ~25°C) Cp,w = 4.18 kJ/kg·K (specific heat of water) Tw,in = temperature of incoming water supply (≈ Twb,in for open tank)
Note on Tw,in: If the supply water temperature equals Twb,in, the Cp,w term becomes zero and the denominator simplifies to just hfg. This is the standard adiabatic approximation. The water sensible heat term is only ~4% of hfg anyway, so ignoring it introduces minimal error.

Why the two methods don't always agree

In a perfect adiabatic cooler, both methods should give the same answer. Deviations arise from:

  • RH sensor drift at high humidity — the biggest source of error. The outlet sensor reads higher than reality at RH > 85%, inflating HRout and Mw,evap in Method 1a.
  • Small heat losses to surroundings — causes a real (small) enthalpy drop across the cooler, slightly breaking the adiabatic assumption.
  • Temperature sensor placement — if the outlet thermometer is not fully mixed with the air stream, Tdb,out may not represent the true mean.

Method 1b (corrected HR) and Method 2 should agree within ~10–15% for a valid trial. Large deviations (>30%) suggest a problematic reading that should be flagged.

Saturation effectiveness

This is the standard metric for comparing evaporative cooler performance across different setups and weather conditions. It is normalised against the maximum possible cooling, making it weather-independent:

η = (Tdb,in − Tdb,out) / (Tdb,in − Twb,in) × 100 [%]

The denominator is the maximum possible temperature drop — limited by the wet-bulb temperature of the incoming air. A perfect cooler (outlet fully saturated at Twb,in) would give η = 100%.

Typical values: 70–85% for commercial pad coolers, 55–70% for basic setups.

Two different setups in different weather can be compared fairly on η alone — a setup with η = 78% on a dry day and η = 78% on a humid day is performing consistently.

Water supply sanity check

The calculated evaporation rate should not significantly exceed the measured water supply rate — water cannot evaporate if it wasn't supplied. A small excess (up to ~10%) is acceptable given measurement uncertainty. A large excess (>30%) suggests the evaporation rate calculation has an error, usually from an inflated HRout.
