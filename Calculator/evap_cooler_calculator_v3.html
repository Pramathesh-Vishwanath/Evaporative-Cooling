Why altitude matters — the Bengaluru correction

Bengaluru sits at ~920 m above sea level. The ASHRAE barometric formula gives Patm ≈ 90.75 kPa — about 10.4% below sea-level standard (101.325 kPa). This is not a small correction:

PropertySea level (101.325 kPa)Bengaluru 920 m (90.75 kPa)Error if ignored
HR @ 28.4°C / 70% RH17.09 g/kg19.15 g/kg+12%
h @ same state72.17 kJ/kg77.41 kJ/kg+7.3%
Air density ρ~1.157 kg/m³~1.017 kg/m³−12%
Specific volume v~0.864 m³/kg~0.983 m³/kg+14%

Using sea-level defaults (as in many online tools and textbook tables) in Bengaluru will underestimate humidity ratio and enthalpy by 7–12%, and overestimate air density by ~12%. All evaporation rate calculations directly inherit this error.

ASHRAE barometric pressure formula

P_atm(Z) = 101.325 × (1 − 2.25577×10⁻⁵ × Z)^5.2559 [kPa] Where Z = elevation above sea level in metres. At Z = 920 m: P_atm = 101.325 × (1 − 2.25577×10⁻⁵ × 920)^5.2559 = 101.325 × (0.97924)^5.2559 = 90.75 kPa

This is from ASHRAE Handbook of Fundamentals, Chapter 1 (Standard Atmosphere). It supersedes the simple approximation P ≈ 101.325 − 0.012 × Z that appears in some older references.

Hyland-Wexler saturation pressure (ASHRAE Ch.1)

Above 0°C, ASHRAE uses the Hyland-Wexler equation for saturation vapour pressure, which is traceable to ITS-90 and matches CoolProp to within display precision across the HVAC range:

ln(P_ws) = C8/T + C9 + C10·T + C11·T² + C12·T³ + C13·ln(T) [Pa, T in Kelvin] C8 = −5.8002206 × 10³ C9 = 1.3914993 C10 = −4.8640239 × 10⁻² C11 = 4.1764768 × 10⁻⁵ C12 = −1.4452093 × 10⁻⁸ C13 = 6.5459673 P_sat(kPa) = exp(ln(P_ws)) / 1000

The simpler Antoine equation (0.6108 × exp(17.27T/(T+237.3))) differs by only 0.04–0.09% in the 25–35°C range, so for most trial accuracy it would not matter. The Hyland-Wexler is used here for full ASHRAE traceability.

Humidity ratio (ASHRAE definition)

HR = 0.622 × P_v / (P_atm − P_v) [kg water / kg dry air] Where: P_v = (RH/100) × P_sat(T_db) [kPa] (actual vapour pressure) 0.622 = M_water / M_dry_air = 18.015 / 28.966 HR_g = HR × 1000 [g/kg]

Note: at lower P_atm (Bengaluru), the denominator (P_atm − P_v) is smaller, so HR is larger for the same T_db and RH. This is why high-altitude air is "thinner" but carries more moisture by mass per kg of dry air.

Specific enthalpy

h = C_p,a × T_db + HR × (h_fg,0 + C_p,v × T_db) [kJ/kg dry air] C_p,a = 1.005 kJ/kg·K C_p,v = 1.805 kJ/kg·K h_fg,0 = 2501 kJ/kg (latent heat at 0°C reference)

Since HR is larger at altitude, enthalpy is also larger. The correction flows automatically once P_atm is correct.

Specific volume and air density

v = R_da × T_K / (P_atm − P_v) [m³/kg dry air] R_da = 0.2870 kJ/(kg·K) (specific gas constant of dry air) T_K = T_db + 273.15 [Kelvin] ρ = 1/v [kg/m³]

At Bengaluru: ρ ≈ 1.017 kg/m³ for typical inlet conditions. Using the often-cited sea-level default of 1.15–1.20 kg/m³ will overestimate mass flow rate M_a = V_a × ρ by ~12–18%, directly inflating all evaporation rate results.

Wet-bulb temperature — iterative Sprung

Twb is solved from the psychrometric equation:

HR = HR_sat(T_wb) − A × (T_db − T_wb) HR_sat(T_wb) = 0.622 × P_sat(T_wb) / (P_atm − P_sat(T_wb)) A = 6.6×10⁻⁴ /°C (Sprung, ventilated psychrometer constant)

Since P_sat(T_wb) is nonlinear in T_wb, this is solved by Newton-Raphson iteration. P_atm appears in HR_sat, so the Bengaluru pressure shifts T_wb as well (by ~0.3–0.5°C relative to sea level). This is equivalent to reading T_wb off an altitude-corrected psychrometric chart.

Dew-point — Magnus inversion

γ = 17.27 × T_db / (T_db + 237.3) + ln(RH/100) T_dp = 237.3 × γ / (17.27 − γ) [°C]

Dew point is independent of P_atm (it depends only on the actual vapour pressure P_v = RH/100 × P_sat, and P_sat depends only on T_db).

Evaporation — Method 1

M_w = (HR_out − HR_in) × M_a [kg/s] → × 1000 × 3600 → [g/hr]

1a uses measured HR_out (suspect); 1b uses corrected HR_out from h = const assumption.

Evaporation — Method 2 (sensible heat balance)

M_w = [M_a × C_p,a × (T_db,in − T_db,out)] / [h_fg + C_p,w × (T_wb,in − T_w,in)]

Twb,in here is the altitude-corrected psychrometric wet-bulb, not a manually entered guess.

Saturation effectiveness

η = (T_db,in − T_db,out) / (T_db,in − T_wb,in) × 100 [%]

Twb,in is computed psychrometrically at Bengaluru altitude — making this a true thermodynamic effectiveness, not an approximation.
